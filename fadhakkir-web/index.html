The time just before Fajr, marking the start of the daily fast during Ramadan. It serves as a preparatory moment to cease eating and drinking, symbolizing discipline, self-restraint, and readiness for spiritual devotion.
The first Islamic prayer of the day, performed at dawn, symbolizing renewal and spiritual connection. It consists of two rak’ahs and is a peaceful time to seek blessings and begin the day with mindfulness and gratitude.
Whoever prays the morning prayer in congregation, then he sits remembering Allah until the sun rises and prays two Rakahs (Duha) will have a reward similar to the Hajj and Umrah pilgrimage. (Tirmidhi)
“He who performs the Fajr will be under the protection of Allah.”(Muslim) For the one who offers the Fardh of Fajr prayer, Allah will protect them the whole day until the next Fajr prayer from harm.
Whoever prays the two cool prayers (Asr and Fajr) will go to Paradise. (Sahih al-Bukhari)
The moment when the sun appears above the horizon, marking the beginning of the day. In Islam, it signals the time for the Fajr prayer and symbolizes new beginnings, spiritual renewal, and the opportunity for reflection and gratitude.
The moment when the sun is at its zenith, directly overhead, and neither inclining east nor west. It marks a brief period during which prayer is not performed, occurring just before the time for Dhuhr begins. Zawaal symbolizes a transitional moment in the day.
The midday prayer in Islam, performed after the sun passes its zenith. It consists of four rak’ahs and provides a moment of spiritual reflection and gratitude amidst the day’s activities, emphasizing the balance between work and worship.
The late afternoon prayer in Islam, performed as the day begins to decline. It consists of four rak’ahs and serves as a reminder of the fleeting nature of time, encouraging reflection, gratitude, and a renewed focus on spiritual and worldly responsibilities.
Whoever prays the two cool prayers (Asr and Fajr) will go to Paradise. (Sahih al-Bukhari)
The daily moment when the sun dips below the horizon, transitioning the sky from day to evening. It symbolizes closure and renewal, marking the time for the Maghrib prayer in Islam and often evoking feelings of peace and reflection.
The evening prayer in Islam, performed just after sunset. It consists of three rak’ahs and marks the transition from day to night, offering a moment of gratitude and reflection as the day concludes.
The final prayer of the day in Islam, performed after the twilight has disappeared and the night has fully set in. It consists of four rak’ahs and serves as a peaceful time for reflection, gratitude, and spiritual connection before rest. Isha is often followed by optional prayers such as the Taraweeh during Ramadan, emphasizing the importance of worship at the end of the day.
In Islam, midnight refers to the midpoint between Isha and Fajr. It is a time for voluntary prayers, such as Tahajjud, offering a peaceful opportunity for reflection, supplication, and closeness to Allah.
